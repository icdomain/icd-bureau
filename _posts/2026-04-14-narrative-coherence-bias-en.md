Just as Communism and Fascism attracted mass support during the Second World War, the general public tends to accept ideas if the narrative holds together. What matters here is not the merits or outcomes of those ideologies.

The central problem is the fact that the public will embrace a coherent story even when its implementation plans and methods remain entirely opaque. In the post-AI-revolution world, this susceptibility to coherent narratives poses a potentially fatal point of intervention for everyone.

Technologies like LLMs have made it trivially easy to attach plausible theory and selective historical fact to any act of violence or exploitation — to dress it up as a well-reasoned argument. Today, it would not be difficult for those in power to disguise rent-seeking as principled theory and push it toward social implementation.

Of course, such theories tend to harbor distortions and excessive complexity between their stated goals and their actual means, so it is possible to identify and point out problems through scrutiny. But such criticism does not change the underlying tendency of the public to be swayed by coherent narratives. Regardless of the quality or truth of the critique, the theory will gain social acceptance, be treated as a matter of consensus, and advance to the implementation stage — and this trajectory may prove impossible to resist.

Therefore, as long as we value freedom, equality, and justice, we will be forced — now more than ever — to think of social decisions and individual internal consent as entirely separate matters.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Yuval Noah Harari, *Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind* (Harper, 2015) — The perspective that treats the cognitive traits of modern humans as adaptations to an environment 200,000 years ago offers an intriguing lens for reinforcing this article's argument. In an era when information was scarce, a coherent theory was precious — and the opportunity it presented was worth the risk of investing in it. One wonders whether that reactive tendency has been inherited by people today. The framework Harari develops invites exactly that inference.
- Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) — Kahneman organizes human judgment into two layers — fast, intuitive judgment and slow, deliberate judgment — and uses extensive experimental data to demonstrate that the former tends to confuse the fluency of information processing with its accuracy. The phenomenon he calls "cognitive ease" can be read as the cognitive-scientific formulation of what this article calls "susceptibility to coherent narratives."
- Philip E. Tetlock, *Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?* (Princeton University Press, 2005) — From over twenty years of tracking expert long-range forecasts, Tetlock demonstrated that experts who hold grand, internally consistent theories tend to have lower predictive accuracy, while experts who adjust their judgments to the complexity of the situation at hand tend to be more accurate. This serves as powerful empirical support for the article's claim that whether a coherent theory actually functions at the implementation stage is an entirely separate question.
- Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — The principal work of a thinker who has repeatedly criticized the gap between the "beautiful theories" constructed by the modern intellectual class and the complexity of reality. The phenomenon in which apparently coherent theories collapse the moment they are implemented in the real world is captured here through the concept of the "Ludic Fallacy." It can be read as a prior critical voice that shares the warning and problem consciousness of this article.
