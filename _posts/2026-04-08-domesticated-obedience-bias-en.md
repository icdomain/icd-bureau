From the earliest times, states and communities have used rituals and ceremonies to require their citizens to conform to prescribed forms. Making the sign of the cross on entering a church, standing and placing a hand over the heart during the national anthem, the order in which guests are seated at a state dinner, the precise choreography of a military salute, the responses spoken during a wedding or a funeral mass — in all of these, what is asked is that you match the form first and ask about its meaning later, if at all. Ask "why this gesture?" or "why in this order?" and you will rarely get a clear answer. Break the form in the moment, however, and you will unmistakably stand out. What is being demanded is obedience without understanding the purpose, or at the very least outward compliance even when one is not inwardly convinced.

To the person doing it, this does not feel like a loss of self. It is "just the form," and one has not surrendered one's will or one's opinions as such. It is precisely this escape hatch — it's only the form — that makes the demand easy to accept. Because the coercion is not felt as coercion, the form spreads wide and sinks deep.

But in a society where those who keep to the form are treated as desirable and those who deviate are met with friction or disadvantage, this repetition becomes more than a code of conduct. Those who can conform find work more easily, marry more easily, and are allowed to remain in the community. Those who question the form are regarded as troublesome, are pushed out, or simply lose chances at marriage and advancement. When this difference in treatment accumulates over many generations, what remains in the group are people of the "conform first" type, not people of the "question the meaning" type. This is not purely a matter of biological inheritance. Through what parents teach their children and what they scold them for, behavioral tendencies themselves are handed down to the next generation.

It is worth noting carefully what is being inherited here. What survives is the disposition to keep the form, not the disposition to ask after its meaning or legitimacy. Questioning does not contribute to survival — often it actively harms it. Matching the form comes first; examination of meaning and validity recedes into the background.

When this tendency is reproduced over several generations, the result is a collective disposition — an obedient national character — in which conforming to existing forms feels natural even when those forms are thin in meaning or plainly unreasonable. Individuals may believe they are "thinking for themselves," but the very premises of what they doubt and what they do not doubt have already been shaped for them.

That states and communities needed to train their members toward a certain degree of obedience in order to prevent fragmentation and maintain order is not something I mean to deny. It was, in part, a necessary cost of having a society at all. But the fact that this cost, paid out across many generations, has left us today with this obedience bias internalized — that fact deserves to be recognized.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Émile Durkheim, *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*, trans. Karen E. Fields (Free Press, 1995) — the classic formulation of ritual as a device for social integration.
- Pierre Bourdieu, *The Logic of Practice*, trans. Richard Nice (Stanford University Press, 1990) — the argument that forms inscribed in the body (habitus) govern action beneath the level of conscious thought.
- Roy A. Rappaport, *Ritual and Religion in the Making of Humanity* (Cambridge University Press, 1999) — the analysis that participation in ritual itself constitutes assent to its propositions, and thereby establishes order.
