The AI-driven displacement of white-collar workers will flood the blue-collar labor market. At that point, it is highly likely that capital will, out of strategic rationality, conceal the fact that operations are scalable and profit margins can well afford human workers — staging the appearance of a labor surplus instead.

If AI adoption and robotization are pushed forward while workers languish in poverty — or lose their bargaining power through the fear of it — and the illusion of oversupply is maintained, wages can be driven down and more versatile human laborers secured cheaply.

Once the wage floor has been destroyed and the exploitation of labor at bargain prices has become the norm, plans for housing, infrastructure, urban redevelopment, and space development will surge into motion all at once. Just as many famous kings throughout history built grand tombs and temples, those who scheme to build their own monuments never cease to exist. They are simply waiting for it to become "cheap."

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Naomi Klein, *The Shock Doctrine: The Rise of Disaster Capitalism* (Metropolitan Books, 2007) — A work that traces the history of crisis being exploited by capital as an opportunity to construct extractive structures. The pattern this article describes — staging oversupply to destroy wages and weaponizing the timing of demand release — is isomorphic to Klein's recurring account of the "shock → deregulation → capital concentration" cycle.
- Thomas Piketty, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (Harvard University Press, 2014) — A study that demonstrated, with 300 years of data, how the structure in which the rate of return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth inevitably widens inequality. This article's prediction that the productivity gains from AI adoption will be absorbed by capital and not distributed to workers suggests that the r > g dynamic Piketty identified may accelerate further in an AI-driven economy.
- Thorstein Veblen, *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (Macmillan, 1899) — A classic that analyzed "conspicuous consumption" by the wealthy as an essential motive of economic behavior. This article's thesis — that those who scheme to build tombs and temples will never cease to exist, so long as costs are low enough — is precisely the dynamic of conspicuous consumption that Veblen formalized over 120 years ago, providing the theoretical basis for the claim that demand will never be exhausted as long as human vanity endures.
