The official story is a stirring one: the free world united to defeat totalitarianism. The reality was less heroic. World War II was, among other things, the moment when the United States outmaneuvered its exhausted brother-nation Britain, let the rest of Europe bleed itself white, and walked away as the relative winner — the new hegemon by default.

America had annexed Hawaii in 1898 by methods indistinguishable from textbook colonialism, and had taken the Philippines, Guam, and Puerto Rico in the same year through the Spanish-American War. Yet from the 1940s onward, the same United States repeatedly pressed Britain to shrink or surrender its colonial holdings. The exact intent is hard to pin down, but the pattern is consistent with a deliberate vision: an American-centered world order, achieved if necessary by hollowing out Britain itself.

Textbooks and mainstream histories tend to file this Anglo-American friction under "differences in values" or "tensions within the alliance." But once Hawaii is placed on the timeline, the principled-anti-colonialism reading collapses. It does not survive contact with the dates. What remains is the reading that America's anti-colonial rhetoric was not principle but strategy.

After the war, Washington went further than simply urging Britain and Europe to let go of their colonies. Through education, media, and diplomatic discourse, the United States propagated a historical worldview in which colonialism itself was disowned — and in which not only Japan and Nazi Germany but also the largest colonial empire in history, the British Empire, found itself indirectly in the dock. Exhausted by the war and facing the Soviet threat, Britain and Europe could not afford to push back against the country bankrolling their recovery. Colonialism became taboo with remarkable speed.

The clearest expression of this dynamic was the Suez Crisis of 1956. When Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal, Britain and France joined Israel in a military intervention and won on the battlefield. Washington refused to back them, and applied financial pressure — a run on sterling — to force a British withdrawal. Militarily victorious, financially broken: the episode made it unmistakable that postwar Britain could no longer take imperial action without American permission.

In parallel, the wave of independence movements and revolutions sweeping Asia and Africa made reconquest by the old metropoles essentially impossible. Attempts to hold colonies by force were made in many places after the war, and almost all of them failed in the long run. Modern European self-image often emphasizes the voluntary relinquishment of empire, but it would be more accurate to say that empires were let go in stages, once they had become impossible to hold.

By this combination of means, America rendered the colonial resource base of Britain and Europe unusable, became the relative strong power, and inherited the position of global hegemon. Britain in particular was saddled with the guilt of having been the largest colonial empire of all, and it has been paying the costs of that dismantlement ever since. To name only recent items: compensation for the suppression of the Mau Mau uprising in Kenya (settled in 2013), repatriation demands for museum collections across the former empire, and the social and fiscal burdens of postcolonial migration. These quiet reparations are still being paid.

Eighty years on, the thing worth noting on the anniversary is this: beneath the story of "freedom defeating totalitarianism," what actually happened was simpler and less flattering — one hegemon skillfully neutralized another and took its place.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Correlli Barnett, *The Collapse of British Power* (Eyre Methuen, 1972) — a classic study arguing that Britain's "victory" in WWII was paid for with the economic and strategic collapse of its empire.
- John Darwin, *The Empire Project: The Rise and Fall of the British World-System, 1830–1970* (Cambridge University Press, 2009) — a comprehensive structural analysis of the dismantling of the British Empire, treating both internal decay and external pressure.
- Christopher Layne, *The Peace of Illusions: American Grand Strategy from 1940 to the Present* (Cornell University Press, 2006) — a reading of postwar American grand strategy as the pursuit of hegemony rather than the expression of liberal ideals.
