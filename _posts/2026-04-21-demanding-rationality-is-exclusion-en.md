Demanding rationality of everyone is cruel.

As is true of biological organisms in general, humans are systems that have been refined to live, reproduce, and persist.

Individuals who believed in something as logically wrong as geocentrism nonetheless left vast quantities of information to posterity through trial and error, laid the groundwork that would eventually prove heliocentrism, and undergirded the dawn of modern science.

Or consider the individual who is foolish, yet indifferent to causation, never despairing of the future, and therefore optimistic — the kind of person who endured disasters, epidemics, and the deaths of loved ones with nothing but their own body, and protected their children and families through problems that no theory or reasoning could solve.

The traits and behavioral tendencies labeled foolish in the modern era, when considered against the uncertainty of life and the gap between what can be understood and what can be resolved, are far from easily dismissible as wrong — in many cases, they possess extremely high rationality as systems designed for survival.

From this perspective, humans are not, in the first place, systems designed to solve given tasks rationally. It is therefore a logical inevitability — requiring no statistical evidence to establish — that the majority of humans will be outperformed not merely by AI but by machines in work-like tasks. Post-AI-revolution society may ignore this reality and demand AI-surpassing performance under the banner of reskilling, but the room for that is limited, and failure to adapt is not the individual's fault.

Rather, in a reality where such individuals constitute the majority of the population, deploying AI at scale without guarantees of income or employment, without legal frameworks, and without transition periods risks, over the medium to long term, the mass exclusion of biologically healthy and sound individuals on the basis of task performance.

## Related Reading

The following works develop arguments that structurally overlap with the thesis of this essay.

- Gerd Gigerenzer, *Gut Feelings: The Intelligence of the Unconscious* (Viking, 2007) — A study that empirically demonstrated how human intuitive judgment, in real-world environments where information is incomplete and time is limited, exhibits higher adaptiveness than precise calculation. It provides direct support from cognitive science for this article's claim that "traits deemed foolish possess extremely high rationality as systems for survival."
- Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — A work that examines the properties of systems that benefit from uncertainty and shocks. Its central thesis — that optimization in pursuit of efficiency renders systems fragile, and that seemingly inefficient redundancy is what enables survival — resonates deeply with this article's premise that "humans are not systems designed to solve given tasks rationally."
- Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (Farrar & Rinehart, 1944) — A classic that historically traced the process by which labor was incorporated into markets as a "commodity," arguing that this was not a natural evolution but a politically constructed one. This article's perspective — calling the structure that can only measure human value through task performance a "structural defect of modern society" — lies on the extension of the problematic Polanyi identified as "fictitious commodity."
